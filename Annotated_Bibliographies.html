WHAT IS AN ANNOTATED BIBLIOGRAPHY?

An annotated bibliography is a list of books, articles, and/or other documents that summarizes a scholar’s research or lists important works on a particular subject. Each entry begins with a citation (author, title, publication info, etc.) followed by a brief descriptive paragraph (usually 100-200 words) that summarizes and evaluates the work cited.

ANNOTATIONS vs. ABSTRACTS

Abstracts are descriptive summaries of a work, usually found at the beginning of journal articles or in abstracting indexes, that help the researcher to quickly determine a work’s purpose and scope. Annotations, however, are descriptive and evaluative - offering an outside perspective on the work being cited and addressing the relevance and quality of the work, as well as the authority of the author.

Annotations should be concise and summarize the central theme and scope of the work. They should also Include one or more sentences that (a) evaluate the authority or background of the author, (b) comment on the intended audience, (c) compare or contrast this work with another you have cited, or (d) explain how this work illuminates your bibliography topic.

Here is a checklist to consider:

·         Explanation of the main purpose and scope of the cited work

·         Brief description of the work's format and content

·         Theoretical basis and currency of the author's argument

·         Author's intellectual/academic credentials

·         Work's intended audience

·         Value and significance of the work as a contribution to the subject under consideration

·         Possible shortcomings or bias in the work

·         Any significant special features of the work (e.g., glossary, appendices, particularly good index)

·         Your own brief impression of the work
